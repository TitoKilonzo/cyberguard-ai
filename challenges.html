Four Core Risks

Know the Risks

AI brings new challenges that complement — rather than replace — the need for skilled human cybersecurity professionals and sound organizational policies.

01
Adversarial Attacks Targeting AI
Attackers deliberately craft inputs designed to fool AI models — adversarial examples — causing security systems to misclassify malicious content as benign, creating dangerous blind spots.
02
Bias Present in AI Models
AI trained on biased datasets may generate disproportionate false positives for certain user groups, or completely miss novel attack vectors outside its training distribution.
03
Issues Related to Data Privacy
AI security systems require vast amounts of behavioral data, raising significant concerns about user privacy, data sovereignty, cross-border transfers, and regulatory compliance.
04
Over-Reliance on AI
Excessive dependence on AI without sufficient human supervision creates weaknesses. AI systems could overlook threats beyond their designed parameters, creating critical security gaps.
Adversarial Threats

AI as an Attack Surface

Cybercriminals actively exploit AI models themselves. Understanding how AI can be weaponized is critical to building truly resilient defenses.

Evasion Attacks
Malware engineered to appear benign to AI classifiers by mimicking legitimate process signatures or exploiting model decision boundaries.
Poisoning Attacks
Attackers inject malicious training data to corrupt AI models at the learning stage, causing them to develop systematic blind spots post-deployment.
AI-Generated Phishing
Threat actors use generative AI to produce highly convincing, personalized phishing content at scale, bypassing traditional content filters.
